\begin{abstract}
% ============================================================
In \textit{Magic: The Gathering} (MTG) Commander, players assign each deck a power-level ``bracket'' on a five-point ordinal scale, intended as a coordination signal for casual play. Community calculators produce a parallel rule-based estimate of the same quantity from the decklist alone, and the two readings frequently disagree. Our goal is to understand that disagreement: where it lives, how large it is, and which deck-level signals drive it. To do so, we train predictive models on the community label and use them as probes --- a model that learns to imitate community judgements lets us isolate which deck features the community implicitly reacts to. We use two complementary representations of the same decks as parallel probes. Bag of Cards (BC), a sparse count vector indexed by card identity, asks whether the community label is driven by specific cards. Deck Features (DF), a dense vector of 102 hand-engineered structural attributes, asks whether it is driven by aggregate deck structure. On a dataset of 12{,}135 public Archidekt decks with both an author-assigned bracket \(y_{\text{arch}}\) and an EDHPowerLevel bracket \(y_{\text{calc}}\) in \(\{2,3,4\}\), six classifiers per representation are tuned and evaluated under 3-repeat, 5-fold nested cross-validation, using only \(y_{\text{arch}}\) as the supervised target. DF outperforms BC for every tuned algorithm: the strongest individual model is DF Gradient Boosting at macro-F1 \(0.6908 \pm 0.0093\), and a top-3 hard-voting ensemble reaches \(0.6941 \pm 0.0096\). When we compare the resulting predictions against the calculator (never used at training time), DF Random Forest aligns with \(y_{\text{calc}}\) on 69.3\% of decks, exceeding the 60.9\% agreement between the raw labels. Permutation importance on DF and lift analysis on BC implicate the same signals on both sides --- Game Changer count, mass land denial, combo references, tutors, and a small set of recognisable high-power cards. We do not adjudicate between the community and the calculator; we describe where the two readings agree, where they diverge, and what explains the gap.

\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}
% ============================================================

\textit{Magic: The Gathering} (MTG) is a collectible card game in which players build decks from a large card pool and compete under format-specific rules. Commander, the format studied here, is multiplayer and broadly social: each player brings a 100-card singleton deck led by a legendary creature, and a substantial fraction of the experience is determined before the first turn, in a brief discussion of expected deck power. The format provides no matchmaking; it relies on this pre-game negotiation instead.

Commander Brackets are the official five-level vocabulary used for that discussion. Bracket~1 corresponds to thematic, low-power decks; bracket~5 to competitive EDH (cEDH); brackets~2--4 cover the casual middle where the bulk of negotiation occurs. Authors assign brackets to their own decks, combining explicit rule-based constraints with subjective judgement. Several community calculators offer an alternative: they parse the same decklist and produce a rule-based estimate from card identities, lists of cards flagged as high-impact (Game Changers, tutors, combo pieces), prices, and popularity. The two readings frequently disagree.

The central question is empirical: \textit{how far apart are the two readings, and which deck-level signals account for the gap?} The first half is answered by direct counting (Section~\ref{sec:data:eda}). The second is harder, because the community label is subjective and we have no closed-form description of what the community reacts to. To probe it, we train predictive models on the community label and use them as instruments: a classifier that learns to imitate the author's bracket from observable deck content tells us what the community label is correlated with, and the features the model relies on most heavily are candidates for explaining the gap.

To separate two natural hypotheses about what drives the community label --- specific cards versus aggregate deck structure --- we deliberately use two representations of the same decks in parallel. Bag of Cards (BC) is a sparse count vector indexed by card identity (\(\approx\)11k dimensions); DF is a dense vector of 102 hand-engineered structural attributes (mana curve, card-type balance, Game Changer count, tutor count, combo density). Under BC the model can latch onto staples, combo pieces, and high-impact singletons by name; under DF it sees only aggregated properties of the deck and has no access to card identity. Comparing the two answers a methodologically clean question and feeds directly into the interpretability stage, where the best DF model and the best BC model are inspected separately.

We collected 12{,}135 public Commander decks from Archidekt for which both \(y_{\text{arch}}\) and the EDHPowerLevel bracket \(y_{\text{calc}}\) lie in \(\{2,3,4\}\). A low-cost spot-checking pass over seven algorithms with distinct inductive biases retains six, which are then tuned per representation under 3-repeat, 5-fold nested cross-validation with per-fold preprocessing and shared splits. \(y_{\text{calc}}\) is never used at training time; only \(y_{\text{arch}}\) is the supervised target. The out-of-fold predictions are aligned with \(y_{\text{calc}}\) post hoc, so that we can ask which models, trained only on the community label, end up closest to the calculator. We additionally verify that completeness checks, GroupKFold by commander identity, and paired statistical tests are consistent with the headline ranking.

\paragraph{Research question.} \textit{To what extent do Archidekt-assigned Commander brackets diverge from automatically calculated bracket estimates, and which deck characteristics explain the disagreement?}

\paragraph{Contributions.}
\begin{enumerate}
  \item A curated dataset of 12{,}135 high-visibility Commander decks with both \(y_{\text{arch}}\) and \(y_{\text{calc}}\) labels in \(\{2,3,4\}\), released along with extraction code.
  \item A direct measurement of the raw divergence between \(y_{\text{arch}}\) and \(y_{\text{calc}}\) on the modeling set, including signed distribution and structural correlates.
  \item A comparison of two deck representations (BC and DF) across six classifiers under repeated nested cross-validation, with per-fold preprocessing and shared splits.
  \item A voting analysis over the top 3, 5, and 7 models by macro-F1, plus a separate selection of the best BC and DF models.
  \item A comparison between \(y_{\text{arch}}\), \(\hat{y}_{\text{arch}}\), and \(y_{\text{calc}}\), followed by interpretability analyses for the best DF and BC models.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Background}
\label{sec:background}
% ============================================================

This section introduces only the game context required to follow the data and experimental setup. Game-specific terms used downstream (Game Changers, tutors, combos, EDHREC rank, salt score, layouts, keywords) are defined in Appendix~\ref{app:mtg}.

\subsection{MTG and Commander}
\label{sec:bg:mtg-commander}

MTG is a turn-based collectible card game first published in 1993~\cite{mtg}. Players build decks from a pool of tens of thousands of cards, draw an initial hand, and cast spells by paying their cost in a resource called \textit{mana}, produced primarily by \textit{land} cards. Each non-land card carries a converted mana cost (CMC) and belongs to one of seven primary types (creature, instant, sorcery, artifact, enchantment, planeswalker, land). MTG defines several formats; we restrict our attention to Commander.

Commander, also known as Elder Dragon Highlander (EDH), is defined by three constraints: each deck contains exactly 100 cards, one of which is the \textit{commander} (a legendary creature playable from a separate zone at any point); the other 99 cards must respect the commander's \textit{color identity}; and, except for basic lands, each card may appear at most once per deck (the \textit{singleton} rule). A standard game has four players, each starting at 40 life. Because every player brings a different list, a single table can mix widely different strategies, budgets, and expectations. The format provides no matchmaking; balance comes entirely from the pre-game discussion of expected deck power, and that is what the bracket system is designed to support.

\subsection{Commander Brackets (community label \texorpdfstring{\(y_{\text{arch}}\)}{y\_arch})}
\label{sec:bg:brackets}
The Commander Rules Committee published the five-level bracket system in October 2024~\cite{commanderbrackets}. Bracket~1 corresponds to relaxed, thematic decks; bracket~5 to cEDH, decks tuned to compete with other tuned decks; brackets~2--4 cover the casual middle. We focus on this middle range because it accounts for nearly all of the publicly available data and because the endpoints address qualitatively different use cases.

The bracket rules supply constraints rather than a complete classifier. They cap Game Changer counts and describe broad expectations around speed, consistency, and disruption, but the final assignment remains up to the author --- two players may reasonably assign different brackets to nearly identical decks because they weigh consistency, combo density, local norms, and threat level differently. In our data, \(y_{\text{arch}}\) is the bracket the author selected on Archidekt~\cite{archidekt}.

\subsection{Automated calculators (calculator label \texorpdfstring{\(y_{\text{calc}}\)}{y\_calc})}
\label{sec:bg:calculator}
A small number of community tools accept a decklist as input and return a bracket assignment. In this work we use EDHPowerLevel~\cite{edhpowerlevel}, a public web application whose internal rules are not formally documented; we therefore treat it as a black box. From its outputs and accompanying breakdown screens, the tool clearly reacts to cards on curated lists (Game Changers, tutors, extra-turn effects, mass land denial), to known combo pieces, and to card prices and EDHREC popularity~\cite{edhrec}, but we do not attempt to reverse-engineer the exact aggregation function. What matters for this paper is the input/output behaviour: the calculator observes only the card list (and time-varying external statistics it pulls in), so \(y_{\text{calc}}\) cannot encode player intent, table norms, or piloting skill, and its output changes when prices or popularity move even if the deck does not.

Disagreement between the two labels is common: a deck the author assigned to bracket~3 may receive bracket~2 or 4 from the calculator. Neither label is treated as ground truth --- \(y_{\text{arch}}\) reflects the author's reading and \(y_{\text{calc}}\) reflects one specific rule set --- and our objective is to characterise the misalignment, not to adjudicate between the two sources.

\section{Dataset Construction}
\label{sec:dataset}
% ============================================================

\subsection{Data sources}
\label{sec:data:sources}

Community labels are obtained from \textbf{Archidekt}, a public deck-building platform that stores user-constructed decklists, view counts, and the bracket assigned by each author. Archidekt exposes a paginated REST API, which we used to retrieve raw deck payloads (full mainboard and per-deck metadata).

Calculator labels are obtained from \textbf{EDHPowerLevel}, a web application that takes a pasted decklist and returns a bracket assignment alongside a continuous power score. Because EDHPowerLevel exposes no programmatic API, we automated its interface using a headless Chromium browser driven by Playwright: each decklist was pasted into the page and the resulting bracket was read from the rendered DOM. Both extraction passes operated against public endpoints only, with conservative per-request pacing and no credentials.

\paragraph{Collection window and snapshots.} Collection took place in May 2026. All time-varying signals --- card prices, EDHREC rank, salt score, the curated Game Changers / tutors / mass land denial / atomic combo lists, and \(y_{\text{calc}}\) itself --- are snapshots taken at that point in time, despite the fact that many of the decks under study were created earlier. The resulting temporal mismatch is a known limitation and is discussed in Section~\ref{sec:conclusion:discussion}.

\subsection{Extraction pipeline}
\label{sec:data:extraction}

The Archidekt pass returned 14{,}421 deck records across the brackets we queried. After removing 1{,}467 duplicate IDs (identical decks appearing across multiple paginated pages) and 4 records whose card-list fingerprints collided, 12{,}950 unique decks remained. Each was then submitted to EDHPowerLevel to obtain \(y_{\text{calc}}\) and the auxiliary power score.

The Archidekt query filtered records upstream, so every retained deck already satisfies all of:
\begin{itemize}
  \item public on Archidekt, format Commander;
  \item author-assigned bracket \(y_{\text{arch}} \in \{2,3,4\}\);
  \item at least 1{,}000 views;
  \item exactly 100 cards in the mainboard;
  \item mainboard only (maybeboard, sideboard, tokens, and extras were ignored).
\end{itemize}

Joining \(y_{\text{calc}}\) introduced an additional filter: 815 decks (6.3\%) received \(y_{\text{calc}} \in \{1,5\}\) and were excluded from the supervised setup. These are the sparse tails of the bracket scale --- bracket~1 is reserved for low-power, thematic decks, and bracket~5 corresponds to cEDH --- and are qualitatively distinct from the middle of the spectrum. Retaining them would force the classifier to allocate capacity to a few hundred edge cases rather than to the range where the actual community negotiation takes place. What remains is the \textbf{modeling set of 12{,}135 decks}, with both labels in \(\{2,3,4\}\).

\subsection{Labels}
\label{sec:data:labels}

Each deck carries two labels. The community label \(y_{\text{arch}}\) is the bracket the author assigned on Archidekt; the calculator label \(y_{\text{calc}}\) is what EDHPowerLevel returned for the same card list. Neither label is treated as ground truth at any point in the analysis. The community label is subjective and varies between authors; the calculator label encodes a single deterministic rule set, fed by time-varying inputs (prices, popularity). All supervised models are trained against \(y_{\text{arch}}\) exclusively; \(y_{\text{calc}}\) is joined back after training solely for descriptive comparison.

\subsection{Bag of Cards (BC)}
\label{sec:data:bc}

Each deck is encoded as a sparse vector indexed by the training-fold card vocabulary, with each dimension storing the count of that card in the deck. Under the singleton rule, most non-basic cards appear at most once; only basic lands can occur in multiples.

The vocabulary is rebuilt inside every fold from the training partition alone. Cards unseen during training are dropped at test time, and any card appearing in fewer than 10 training decks is pruned (\texttt{min\_df = 10}). Pruning removes near-zero-variance columns that would otherwise inflate dimensionality without adding usable signal. The cutoff was selected by sweeping $\{5, 10, 20\}$ during spot-checking (Section~\ref{sec:spotcheck}). After pruning, the resulting matrix has approximately 11{,}000 columns.

Crucially, BC keeps card identity visible to the model. Staples, combo pieces, and high-impact singletons all appear as their own dimensions, so the model is free to associate individual cards with the community bracket.

\subsection{Deck Features (DF)}
\label{sec:data:df}

DF encodes each deck as a dense vector of 105 structural attributes extracted from the Archidekt JSON payload. Features were selected by hand to capture deck construction rather than authoring style. Each attribute belongs to a familiar Commander-relevant category: mana curve, card-type balance, combo density, price, popularity, bracket-specific flags, and so on. Three attributes (\texttt{has\_companion}, \texttt{mainboard\_count}, \texttt{two\_card\_combo\_singleton\_count}) are constant within the modeling subset and are eliminated by the fold-fitted zero-variance filter, leaving 102 features ultimately presented to each classifier. The complete catalog, with the exact computation for each attribute, is provided in Appendix~\ref{app:features}.

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Mana base and curve.} Land count, basic / non-basic split, mana production per color, and CMC summary statistics plus per-bucket counts.
  \item \textbf{Card types.} Counts of creatures, instants, sorceries, artifacts, enchantments, planeswalkers, and non-land permanents.
  \item \textbf{Bracket-relevant flags.} Counts of Game Changers, tutors, extra-turn effects, and mass land denial cards --- the four signals the bracket system uses to set hard caps.
  \item \textbf{Combo density.} Cards involved in atomic combos, in two-card combos, and total combo references.
  \item \textbf{Popularity and price.} EDHREC rank statistics, salt score statistics, total and per-card deck price.
  \item \textbf{Rarity.} Counts of common, uncommon, rare, and mythic cards.
  \item \textbf{Keywords.} Counts of evergreen keywords, total and distinct keyword counts.
  \item \textbf{Colors and identity.} Color count and per-color presence flags.
  \item \textbf{Multiface layouts.} Counts of transform, modal double-faced, split, and adventure cards.
\end{itemize}

Features derived from free-text user input are deliberately excluded. The Archidekt deck editor lets the author group cards under \textit{categories}, which appear in the payload as arbitrary user-typed strings rather than a fixed vocabulary: a card that one author tags as \textit{Removal} another may tag as \textit{Interaction}, \textit{Wipe}, or simply \textit{Removal Suite}, and a third may not tag at all. An audit found that 22.8\% of decks rely solely on author-defined labels that do not map back to the standard functional vocabulary. Treating those strings as features would predominantly encode authoring style (which tag taxonomy the author happens to prefer) rather than properties of the deck itself, so we drop them.

\subsection{Hold-out protocol}
\label{sec:spotcheck:protocol}

Each candidate is run on both representations with scikit-learn 1.5 defaults (Appendix~\ref{app:spotcheck} lists them explicitly). We use 5 stratified 80/20 hold-out splits with seeds $\{1, 2, 3, 4, 5\}$, stratified by $y_{\text{arch}}$, and report the mean and standard deviation of macro-F1 across seeds. Spot-checking is deliberately not a tuning pass: leaving hyperparameters fixed at their defaults ensures that the resulting ranking reflects the algorithm itself, not the amount of effort invested in its grid.

\section{Training and Model Selection}
\label{sec:training}
% ============================================================

\subsection{Nested cross-validation}
\label{sec:training:nestedcv}

Nested cross-validation is used to keep hyperparameter selection separated from the final estimate of generalization performance:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Outer loop.} \texttt{StratifiedKFold} with 5 splits, repeated with 3 independent seeds (1, 2, 3), yielding 15 outer evaluations per model.
  \item \textbf{Inner loop.} \texttt{StratifiedKFold} with 3 splits ($\text{seed} = \text{outer\_seed} + 100$). The inner loop performs the grid search, selecting the best configuration for each outer fold without ever observing the corresponding outer test set.
\end{itemize}

All 12 models share the same outer and inner fold indices. Indices are derived from the label vector and the seeds at runtime, so any machine with the same dataset and dependency versions reproduces the same splits. Because every model is evaluated on the same held-out rows, paired statistical tests across models are well-defined.

\subsection{Per-fold preprocessing}
\label{sec:training:preprocessing}

All preprocessing is fit on the training partition of the current fold and applied to the held-out partition without ever observing it. No statistic is computed on the full dataset prior to splitting, so test-fold information cannot leak into the model through imputation values, outlier caps, or scaling parameters. The full rationale (median imputation, winsorization of price, and the selective application of scaling) is provided in Appendix~\ref{app:preprocessing}; in summary:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Imputation.} Missing EDHREC ranks and salt scores are filled with the training-fold median.
  \item \textbf{Winsorization.} \texttt{price\_total} is clipped at the training-fold 99th percentile; the long tail (a handful of decks above \$50{,}000) would otherwise dominate linear models and waste tree splits.
  \item \textbf{Scaling.} \texttt{StandardScaler} is applied to DF features for the scale-sensitive algorithms (Logistic Regression, Linear SVC, KNN). Tree-based models and Gaussian Naive Bayes receive unscaled DF features.
  \item \textbf{Leakage prevention.} The model sees only the current training partition. All fields derived from EDHPowerLevel (\(y_{\text{calc}}\), the continuous power score, and any column prefixed \texttt{epl\_}) are stripped from every feature matrix, in every fold. The only training target is \(y_{\text{arch}}\); \(y_{\text{calc}}\) is joined back after training, only for descriptive comparison.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Evaluation metrics}
\label{sec:training:metrics}

The primary metric is macro-F1, which averages per-class F1 with equal weight across classes. This choice is important here because bracket~3 accounts for 51.2\% of the modeling set, and plain accuracy would obscure poor recall on the minority classes. We additionally report accuracy, macro-precision, macro-recall, and per-class confusion matrices, all as mean $\pm$ standard deviation over the 15 outer folds.

\section{Model-Calculator Comparison}
\label{sec:modelcalc}
% ============================================================

This section is purely descriptive: no model is trained or retrained, and \(y_{\text{calc}}\) is never used as a target. The analysis reads the out-of-fold predictions saved by nested CV and voting, and compares them to the calculator label on the same deck rows.

Since the outer evaluation uses 3 repeats over the 12{,}135-deck modeling set, the OOF comparison contains 36{,}405 rows. Each deck has a stable \(y_{\text{calc}}\) but contributes one prediction per repeat; for this reason, the OOF confusion matrices in the appendix sum to 36{,}405 rather than 12{,}135.

Two baselines are used. The first is the direct label comparison \(y_{\text{arch}}\) versus \(y_{\text{calc}}\), already shown in Table~\ref{tab:yarch-ycalc-matrix}. The second is the model comparison \(\hat{y}_{\text{arch}}\) versus \(y_{\text{calc}}\). We report exact agreement, within-one-bracket agreement, mean absolute difference, and macro-F1 against \(y_{\text{calc}}\). Exact agreement answers the question of how often each source matches the calculator; macro-F1 against \(y_{\text{calc}}\) answers the same question while accounting for class imbalance. The gap column has a different interpretation: it is defined as \(|\mathrm{macro\text{-}F1}(y_{\text{arch}}) - \mathrm{exact\ agreement}(\hat{y}_{\text{arch}}, y_{\text{calc}})|\), and quantifies how far a model's performance on the Archidekt target sits from its raw agreement with the calculator. A large gap indicates that a model is a strong predictor of \(y_{\text{arch}}\) without being the one closest to \(y_{\text{calc}}\).

The table is dense; we read it through four questions.

\paragraph{Q1. Which representation gets closer to the community?} DF, by a wide margin. Table~\ref{tab:nested-results} shows DF outperforming BC on \(y_{\text{arch}}\) for every algorithm; the strongest DF model beats the strongest BC model by +0.0475 macro-F1. The community label is more about \textit{what kind of deck this is} than about \textit{which specific cards are in it}.

\paragraph{Q2. Which representation gets closer to the calculator?} Also DF. Every model that exceeds the raw 60.9\% baseline in the Exact column of Table~\ref{tab:calc-alignment} is a DF model (DF Random Forest at 69.3\%, top-3 vote at 66.7\%, DF Gradient Boosting at 64.0\%); BC Gradient Boosting nearly matches the raw labels (60.3\%) and other BC models sit below. This is partially circular --- DF counts the same kinds of cards EDHPowerLevel counts (Section~\ref{sec:conclusion:discussion}) --- but informative: DF has no feature for which specific Game Changer is present, so the alignment is driven by aggregates, not identity.

\paragraph{Q3. Which algorithm aligns most with the community vs the calculator?} Not the same one. The best community predictor is DF Gradient Boosting (macro-F1 0.6908 on \(y_{\text{arch}}\), but only 64.0\% agreement with the calculator --- the largest gap in the table). The best calculator-aligned model is DF Random Forest (69.3\% agreement, macro-F1 0.6733 on \(y_{\text{arch}}\) --- 0.0175 below the best). Gradient Boosting is willing to over- or under-shoot the calculator when that improves community-label accuracy; Random Forest's bagged majority vote lands closer to the calculator's rule-driven behaviour even though both were trained on \(y_{\text{arch}}\).

\paragraph{Q4. What does the gap mean?} A high gap (DF Gradient Boosting, 0.0508) marks a model that captures community-specific structure not aligned with the calculator's rules. A low gap (BC Naive Bayes, 0.0010) marks a weak classifier roughly equally bad at both targets --- not one that has learned the calculator. Closeness to the calculator is informative only conditional on first being good at \(y_{\text{arch}}\). Together: the community label and the calculator react to overlapping but not identical structure; deck-level aggregates explain most of the overlap, and the residual --- where DF Gradient Boosting's gap lives --- is what the community sees and the calculator does not.

\section{Interpretability}
\label{sec:interpretability}
% ============================================================

Interpretability is performed on exactly the best individual model per representation, as selected in Section~\ref{sec:training:selection}: DF Gradient Boosting and BC Gradient Boosting. The per-representation split is deliberate: the best global model is a DF model and the best ensemble combines several estimators, yet the paper needs to characterise both representations. We therefore consider two representation-specific questions. For DF, which handpicked deck attributes does the best structured model rely on? For BC, which card identities are enriched in each predicted bracket?

The reported performance estimates remain those from nested CV. For interpretation only, each selected model is fit again on the full modeling set or on a dedicated interpretability split, depending on the method. These refits are not used to claim new predictive performance.

\subsection{Deck Features}
\label{sec:interpretability:df}

For DF, we compute permutation importance on a stratified 20\% hold-out with 10 repeats. HistGradientBoosting does not expose impurity-based feature importances, and permutation importance measures the drop in macro-F1 after shuffling a feature column. A large value indicates that the model loses predictive performance when that feature is decoupled from the label. Table~\ref{tab:df-interpretability} reports the top features.

When clear bracket-defining signals are present, the model and calculator tend to agree; divergence is concentrated in decks whose power level depends on context, synergy, or author interpretation rather than on the explicit bracket flags.

\subsection{Bag of Cards}
\label{sec:interpretability:bc}

\[
\mathrm{lift}(k,c) = \frac{P(c \text{ present}\mid \hat{y}_{\text{arch}}=k)}{P(c \text{ present})}.

Cards present in fewer than 10 unique decks are filtered. \section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}
% ============================================================

This paper has set up a reproducible pipeline for studying the divergence between community-assigned and automatically calculated Commander brackets. On 12{,}135 Archidekt decks with both labels in brackets 2--4, raw agreement between \(y_{\text{arch}}\) and \(y_{\text{calc}}\) is 60.9\%, and the calculator assigns higher brackets more often than authors do when the two disagree. Across six algorithms and two representations, DF outperforms BC under nested cross-validation; the strongest individual model is DF Gradient Boosting at macro-F1 \(0.6908 \pm 0.0093\), and the best voting ensemble improves this only marginally to 0.6941.

Interpretability supports a coherent reading of the results. The DF model is dominated by aggregate structure --- Game Changer count, mass land denial, combo references, and tutors --- while BC lift surfaces the cards that a Commander player would already recognise as bracket-4 indicators: \textit{Blood Moon}, \textit{Chrome Mox}, \textit{Imperial Seal}, and \textit{Mana Vault}. The models do not adjudicate between the community and the calculator; they expose where the two readings overlap and where they diverge.

\subsection{Discussion, Limitations, and Future Work}
\label{sec:conclusion:discussion}

The headline empirical findings are: structured deck features (DF) predict the community bracket better than raw card identity (BC) for every tuned algorithm; the model best aligned with the calculator (DF Random Forest, 69.3\%) is not the model best at predicting the community label (DF Gradient Boosting, macro-F1 0.6908); and the same set of deck-level signals --- Game Changers, mass land denial, combos, tutors, and a handful of recognisable high-power cards --- shows up in both interpretability views. We did not treat either label as ground truth; the contribution is descriptive.

\paragraph{Limitations.} The evidence is bounded in several ways.

\textit{One community source, one calculator.} The community label comes from Archidekt alone and \(y_{\text{calc}}\) from EDHPowerLevel alone. A different platform's users may follow different self-assessment conventions, and other calculators apply different rule sets, so the specific alignment numbers in Table~\ref{tab:calc-alignment} would shift if rerun against a different tool. The directional finding --- DF aggregates align more strongly than BC card identities --- is likely robust, but the 69.3\% is not.

\textit{The two representations are evaluated separately.} BC and DF are deliberately treated as parallel probes and never concatenated. A model that sees both card identity and aggregate structure jointly could reasonably outperform either, but that would conflate the two probes we want to compare.

\textit{Sampling frame.} The Archidekt query retains only decks with at least 1{,}000 views, biasing the sample toward polished, public-facing lists. Lowering the threshold to e.g.~500 would enlarge but also dilute it, which is its own bias rather than a strict improvement.

\textit{Shared feature space with the calculator.} The DF representation explicitly counts signals that the bracket rules and EDHPowerLevel also count: Game Changers, tutors, mass land denial, extra-turn effects, combo references. Even though \(y_{\text{calc}}\) is never used at training time, part of the DF-vs-calculator alignment in Section~\ref{sec:modelcalc} is structurally expected. We flag this as a circularity caveat rather than a corrected number.

\textit{Ordinal brackets, unordered loss.} Brackets are ordinal (2 \(<\) 3 \(<\) 4), but our classifiers treat them as unordered classes; macro-F1 weights a 2~\(\to\)~4 confusion the same as a 2~\(\to\)~3 one. The within-one-bracket agreement we report partially compensates.

\textit{Temporal snapshot.} \(y_{\text{calc}}\) and several DF features (prices, EDHREC rank, salt, curated lists) are May 2026 snapshots; decks were built over a longer span. Random CV splits do not control for this mismatch, and a re-query audit on 90 decks showed 3 calculator-label flips after several weeks.

\textit{Data redistribution.} We re-share only deck IDs, both bracket labels, and derived features --- not raw upstream payloads. Long-term reproducibility against the live sites is not guaranteed; we distribute a frozen snapshot together with the extraction code.

\textit{Bracket prediction is not win-rate prediction.} The models learn labels attached to decklists, not the strength of those decks in actual play.

\paragraph{Future work.} The most informative next experiments are local extensions, not new models. (i)~A circularity ablation that drops the explicit bracket-rule columns from DF and re-runs the calculator-alignment analysis, separating ``DF agrees because it counts the same things'' from ``DF agrees because the community uses the same signals''. (ii)~A second calculator and a second community source (Commander Salt, Moxfield) to test how much of the gap is platform-specific. (iii)~Ordinal-aware modelling and metrics (ordinal logistic regression, MAE on bracket values, quadratic-weighted kappa). (iv)~A BC+DF model that uses card identity and aggregate structure jointly. (v)~A separate experimental design for brackets~1 and~5.

\section{MTG Terminology and Gameplay}
\label{app:mtg}
% ============================================================

This appendix pulls together the MTG concepts that appear in the body. Readers already familiar with the game can skip ahead; the main text is self-contained on methodology and results.

\subsection{Game structure}
\label{app:mtg:structure}

\paragraph{Commander and command zone.} The \textit{commander} is a \textit{legendary creature} that begins the game in a separate zone, the \textit{command zone}, and may be cast from there at any point during the game. The commander's \textit{color identity} --- the set of mana colors appearing in its cost and rules text --- constrains which cards the other 99 cards of the deck may include.

\paragraph{Singleton format.} Commander is a \textit{singleton} format: each deck may contain at most one copy of any non-basic land card. \textit{Basic lands} --- generic resource-producing cards with no unique rules text --- may be included in any quantity.

\paragraph{Player count.} Commander is typically played in pods of four, though the format admits any number of players from two upward. The four-player pod is the configuration around which the bracket discourse is framed.

\subsection{Cards, mana, and the curve}
\label{app:mtg:cards}

\paragraph{Mana and mana cost.} \textit{Mana} is the resource in MTG, produced mostly by \textit{land} cards. Players play at most one land per turn and spend mana to pay the \textit{mana cost} of spells. The \textit{converted mana cost} (CMC), also called \textit{mana value}, is the total amount of mana required to cast a card, regardless of color.

\paragraph{Mana curve.} The distribution of CMC values across a deck is the \textit{mana curve}. Cards with CMC 0--1 are cheap and fast; cards with CMC 5+ are expensive and slow. The shape of the curve reflects the deck's expected speed and consistency.

\paragraph{Card types.} MTG defines several primary \textit{card types}: \textit{creatures} attack and block; \textit{instants} and \textit{sorceries} are one-time spells; \textit{artifacts} are objects with persistent effects; \textit{enchantments} are ongoing magical effects; \textit{planeswalkers} are recurring-ability allies; \textit{lands} produce mana. Cards that remain on the battlefield after being played are collectively called \textit{permanents}.

\paragraph{Rarity tiers.} MTG cards are printed at four rarity levels that broadly correlate with power and availability: \textit{common} (most frequent), \textit{uncommon}, \textit{rare}, and \textit{mythic rare} (least frequent). A deck's rarity distribution is a rough proxy for power ceiling and price.

\paragraph{Multiface layouts.} Some MTG cards have multiple faces. \textit{Transform} cards flip between two faces under specific conditions. \textit{Modal double-faced cards} let the player choose which face to play at cast time. \textit{Split cards} contain two independent spells on one card. \textit{Adventure} cards bundle a creature with an instant or sorcery.

\paragraph{Mana colors.} MTG uses five colors of mana: \textit{White} (W), \textit{Blue} (U), \textit{Black} (B), \textit{Red} (R), and \textit{Green} (G), each associated with a distinct strategic identity.

\subsection{Power indicators}
\label{app:mtg:power}

\paragraph{Staples.} A \textit{staple} is an informal term for a card so generically efficient that it appears in a large fraction of competitive or semi-competitive decks regardless of strategy. Examples: \textit{Sol Ring} (a cheap mana-producing artifact), \textit{Command Tower} (a land that produces any color in the commander's identity).

\paragraph{Game Changers.} \textit{Game Changers} are cards officially designated by the Commander Rules Committee as capable of ending games or dominating tables on their own. Examples: \textit{Thassa's Oracle}, \textit{Rhystic Study}. Their count in a deck is the primary signal the bracket system uses to distinguish brackets 3 and 4.

\paragraph{Tutors.} A \textit{tutor} is a card that searches the player's library for another specific card and places it in the player's hand or in play, raising deck consistency. Examples: \textit{Demonic Tutor}, \textit{Enlightened Tutor}.

\paragraph{Combos.} A \textit{combo} is a combination of two or more cards that, once assembled, produces a game-winning or overwhelming effect (infinite damage, infinite mana, deck-out). An \textit{atomic combo} is one in which every piece is necessary and sufficient; a \textit{two-card combo} is a particularly efficient variant requiring only two pieces.

\paragraph{Extra-turn effects.} Cards that grant a player additional turns beyond the standard cycle (\textit{Time Warp}, \textit{Temporal Manipulation}) create significant tempo advantages and are considered high-impact.

\paragraph{Mass land denial.} Cards that destroy or otherwise neutralize all players' lands simultaneously (\textit{Armageddon}, \textit{Ravages of War}) are socially contentious in Commander because they can render the game unplayable for the rest of the table.

\subsection{Keywords}
\label{app:mtg:keywords}

\textit{Keywords} are shorthand labels for recurring rules text. \textit{Evergreen} keywords appear across many sets and are always available; examples include \textit{Flying} (can be blocked only by creatures with Flying or Reach), \textit{Trample} (excess combat damage carries over to the defending player), \textit{Haste} (can attack the turn it enters play), \textit{Hexproof} (cannot be targeted by opponents), and \textit{Deathtouch} (destroys any creature it damages).

\subsection{Community resources}
\label{app:mtg:community}

\paragraph{EDHREC rank.} EDHREC (\url{https://edhrec.com}) aggregates published Commander decklists and computes per-card popularity statistics. The \textit{EDHREC rank} orders cards by inclusion frequency: rank 1 is the most widely played card, with higher numbers denoting less popular cards.

\paragraph{Salt score.} The \textit{salt score} is a community-sourced metric compiled by EDHREC that measures how much frustration a card is reported to cause opposing players. High-salt cards typically lock opponents out, induce repeated random effects, or end games in ways perceived as unfun.

\section{Preprocessing --- Extended Rationale}
\label{app:preprocessing}
% ============================================================

Section~\ref{sec:training:preprocessing} lists the four transformations used inside nested CV. This appendix is the why behind each one.

\paragraph{Imputation.} EDHREC rank and salt score come from an external community service, and they are missing for cards that EDHREC has not yet indexed (mostly rare or newly released ones). The missing rate is small. We fill those values with the training-fold median because the missingness is a property of the card, not the deck. The median is a better default than the mean because both fields have heavy-tailed distributions (a few cards sit at very high ranks or salt scores); it is also better than zero because zero is not neutral on either scale (EDHREC rank 0 would be the single most popular card, salt 0 a card nobody finds frustrating).

\paragraph{Winsorization.} Total deck price (\texttt{price\_total}) is clipped at the training-fold 99th percentile. Most decks cost a few hundred dollars, but a small collector-grade tail breaks past \$50{,}000 thanks to a handful of reserved-list cards. Without clipping, scale-sensitive linear models would over-weight that tail, and tree splits would lock onto degenerate thresholds set by a single deck. Winsorization keeps the useful signal and trims the leverage. We picked it over a log transform because the unit stays ``dollars,'' which makes coefficients and feature importances easier to read downstream.

\paragraph{Scaling.} \texttt{StandardScaler} centers each DF feature to zero mean and unit variance, fit on the training fold. It is applied to Logistic Regression, Linear SVC, and KNN, where objective functions or distance metrics are scale-dependent and would otherwise be dominated by features like \texttt{price\_total} or \texttt{edhrec\_rank\_mean}. Tree-based models (Decision Tree, Random Forest, Gradient Boosting) and Gaussian Naive Bayes are invariant to monotonic per-feature rescaling, so they get unscaled features and we avoid a transform that would only blur feature-importance interpretation.

\paragraph{Leakage prevention.} The supervised target is $y_{\text{arch}}$. Every column the EDHPowerLevel pass produced ($y_{\text{calc}}$, the continuous power score, efficiency, tipping point, anything prefixed \texttt{epl\_}) is stripped from the feature matrix before fitting. The calculator label is only re-joined for the descriptive comparison after training. Without this rule, a trivial classifier that just copied the EDHPowerLevel bracket would score well while explaining nothing about the Archidekt label.

\section{Deck Features (DF) Catalog}
\label{app:features}
% ============================================================

\begingroup
\sloppy

This appendix walks through every feature in the Deck Features representation from Section~\ref{sec:data:df}, grouped into the same families. Each one is computed strictly from data already in the Archidekt raw payload (mainboard rows, oracle card metadata, printing-level rarity and price); nothing external is queried at training or inference time. Per-card quantities are respected everywhere: a feature defined as a count multiplies the per-card boolean by the row's \texttt{quantity}, which matters for basic lands and the handful of cards allowed in multiples.

\subsection{Basic structure}
\label{app:features:basic}
\begin{itemize}
  \item \texttt{unique\_card\_count}: number of distinct \texttt{oracle\_uid}s in the mainboard.
  \item \texttt{non\_commander\_card\_count}: total quantity of mainboard cards that are not the commander.
  \item \texttt{commander\_card\_count}: total quantity of commander cards (1 for single-commander decks, 2 for Partner pairs and Background combinations).
  \item \texttt{mainboard\_count}: total quantity of mainboard cards. Always 100 in the modeling set (the Commander deck-size rule) and removed by the zero-variance filter; included in the feature payload as a structural sanity check.
  \item \texttt{has\_companion}: boolean flag indicating whether a Companion card is registered for the deck. Always false in the modeling set because Companions are excluded upstream; removed by the zero-variance filter.
  \item \texttt{view\_count}: cumulative number of Archidekt page views for the deck snapshot, used as a coarse popularity proxy.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Colors and color identity}
\label{app:features:colors}
\begin{itemize}
  \item \texttt{deck\_color\_count}: cardinality of the union of \texttt{colorIdentity} across all mainboard cards, in $\{0,\dots,5\}$.
  \item \texttt{has\_W}, \texttt{has\_U}, \texttt{has\_B}, \texttt{has\_R}, \texttt{has\_G}: boolean flags, one per color, true if any card in the mainboard has that color in its identity.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Mana base}
\label{app:features:mana}
\begin{itemize}
  \item \texttt{land\_count}, \texttt{nonland\_count}: total quantities of lands and non-lands in the mainboard.
  \item \texttt{basic\_land\_count}, \texttt{nonbasic\_land\_count}: split of the land count between basic lands (the five color basics plus Wastes, or any card with \texttt{superType=Basic} and \texttt{type=Land}) and non-basics.
  \item \texttt{land\_mana\_production\_W}, \texttt{\_U}, \texttt{\_B}, \texttt{\_R}, \texttt{\_G}, \texttt{\_C}: for each color (including colorless), the count of mainboard lands whose \texttt{manaProduction} entry includes that color.
  \item \texttt{lands\_that\_produce\_multiple\_colors\_count}: count of lands that produce more than one colored mana (colorless not counted).
  \item \texttt{lands\_that\_produce\_colorless\_count}: count of lands whose \texttt{manaProduction} includes the colorless ``C'' symbol.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Mana curve (CMC)}
\label{app:features:curve}
CMC is taken from the canonical \texttt{oracleCard.cmc} field. Archidekt's user-overridable \texttt{custom\_cmc} is deliberately ignored: an audit showed that about 19\% of decks use it on at least one row, mixing legitimate cases (X-spells) with subjective preference, so using it would inject author-specific noise into a feature that is meant to describe the card.
\begin{itemize}
  \item \texttt{cmc\_mean}, \texttt{cmc\_median}, \texttt{cmc\_std}: mean, median, and population standard deviation of CMC across all mainboard cards (lands counted at CMC 0).
  \item \texttt{nonland\_cmc\_mean}, \texttt{nonland\_cmc\_median}, \texttt{nonland\_cmc\_std}: same statistics restricted to non-land cards.
  \item \texttt{cmc\_0\_count}, \texttt{cmc\_1\_count}, \dots, \texttt{cmc\_5\_count}, \texttt{cmc\_6\_plus\_count}: histogram of non-land cards by integer CMC bucket, with CMCs of 6 or more collapsed into a single bucket.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Card types}
\label{app:features:types}
\begin{itemize}
  \item \texttt{creature\_count}, \texttt{instant\_count}, \texttt{sorcery\_count}, \texttt{artifact\_count}, \texttt{enchantment\_count}, \texttt{planeswalker\_count}: count of mainboard cards (respecting quantity) whose \texttt{types} array contains the corresponding type.
  \item \texttt{noncreature\_spell\_count}: count of mainboard cards that are neither creatures nor lands.
  \item \texttt{nonland\_permanent\_count}: count of mainboard cards that are non-land permanents (creature, artifact, enchantment, or planeswalker).
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Bracket-relevant flags}
\label{app:features:bracket}
These count cards flagged by external sources that the bracket system uses to set hard caps (Appendix~\ref{app:mtg:power}). The four boolean flags come directly from the \texttt{oracleCard} payload Archidekt returned at collection time (May 2026); they are not recomputed from Rules Committee documents and not refreshed across folds, so they are a single dataset-wide snapshot. As far as we can tell, Archidekt's curated lists overlap heavily with EDHPowerLevel's because both trace back to the official Commander Rules Committee designation, but we did not audit the two for exact equality. This shared lineage is part of the circularity caveat in Section~\ref{sec:conclusion:discussion}.
\begin{itemize}
  \item \texttt{game\_changer\_count}: count of cards with \texttt{oracleCard.gameChanger = true}.
  \item \texttt{extra\_turns\_count}: count of cards with \texttt{oracleCard.extraTurns = true}.
  \item \texttt{tutor\_count}: count of cards with \texttt{oracleCard.tutor = true}.
  \item \texttt{mass\_land\_denial\_count}: count of cards with \texttt{oracleCard.massLandDenial = true}.
  \item \texttt{two\_card\_combo\_singleton\_count}: count of cards flagged as a singleton piece of a known two-card combo by Archidekt's combo registry. Empirically zero across the entire collected dataset (the underlying \texttt{twoCardComboSingelton} flag is never set on the cards Archidekt returns for our deck pool), so removed by the zero-variance filter.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Combo density}
\label{app:features:combos}
Combo membership is read from Archidekt's combo registry embedded in the deck payload at collection time (May 2026). We do not re-derive combos against the Commander Spellbook database; we use whatever combo references Archidekt happens to attach to each card. Like the bracket flags above, this is a frozen snapshot for the whole dataset.
\begin{itemize}
  \item \texttt{cards\_with\_atomic\_combos\_count}, \texttt{cards\_with\_potential\_combos\_count}: number of mainboard cards that participate, respectively, in at least one atomic combo or at least one potential combo according to Archidekt's combo data.
  \item \texttt{atomic\_combo\_refs\_total}, \texttt{potential\_combo\_refs\_total}: total references summed across cards (a card that participates in three combos contributes 3).
  \item \texttt{unique\_atomic\_combo\_refs\_count}, \texttt{unique\_potential\_combo\_refs\_count}: number of distinct combo identifiers referenced by the deck.
  \item \texttt{two\_card\_combo\_ids\_total}: total references across cards to two-card combos.
  \item \texttt{unique\_two\_card\_combo\_ids\_count}: number of distinct two-card combo identifiers referenced by the deck.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Popularity, salt, and price}
\label{app:features:popularity}
All three families drift over time, and we use a single collection-time snapshot (May 2026) for the whole dataset rather than per-deck values from each deck's creation date. Cross-validation does not fix this temporal mismatch; see Section~\ref{sec:conclusion:discussion}.
\begin{itemize}
  \item \texttt{edhrec\_rank\_mean}, \texttt{edhrec\_rank\_median}, \texttt{edhrec\_rank\_std}: aggregate statistics of EDHREC rank across mainboard cards. Cards with missing EDHREC rank are excluded from these statistics; the resulting missing value is imputed downstream by the fold-fitted median (Appendix~\ref{app:preprocessing}).
  \item \texttt{salt\_mean}, \texttt{salt\_median}, \texttt{salt\_std}: aggregate statistics of EDHREC salt score, treated analogously.
  \item \texttt{price\_total}, \texttt{price\_mean}, \texttt{price\_median}, \texttt{price\_std}: aggregate statistics of paper non-foil price in USD. Vendor preference is TCGPlayer market, falling through Card Kingdom, CardMarket, Star City Games, MagicPlayer, and CardTrader. \texttt{price\_total} is winsorized at the training-fold 99th percentile before modeling.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Rarity}
\label{app:features:rarity}
\begin{itemize}
  \item \texttt{common\_count}, \texttt{uncommon\_count}, \texttt{rare\_count}, \texttt{mythic\_count}: counts of mainboard cards by printing-level rarity, taken from the specific printing the deck uses.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Keywords}
\label{app:features:keywords}
Keywords are extracted from \texttt{oracleCard.keywords}.
\begin{itemize}
  \item \texttt{keyword\_total\_count}: total keyword occurrences across the mainboard (a card with two keywords contributes 2).
  \item \texttt{distinct\_keyword\_count}: number of distinct keyword strings observed in the mainboard.
  \item \texttt{flying\_count}, \texttt{trample\_count}, \texttt{haste\_count}, \texttt{hexproof\_count}, \texttt{ward\_count}, \texttt{indestructible\_count}, \texttt{lifelink\_count}, \texttt{menace\_count}, \texttt{vigilance\_count}, \texttt{deathtouch\_count}, \texttt{flash\_count}, \texttt{equip\_count}, \texttt{annihilator\_count}, \texttt{cascade\_count}: per-keyword card counts for the tracked evergreen and high-impact keywords.
  \item \texttt{protection\_keyword\_count}: count of cards whose keyword list contains any \texttt{Protection from \dots} variant.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Super- and subtypes}
\label{app:features:subtypes}
\begin{itemize}
  \item \texttt{legendary\_count}, \texttt{snow\_count}: counts of cards with the corresponding supertype.
  \item \texttt{distinct\_subtype\_count}: number of distinct subtypes observed across the mainboard.
  \item \texttt{most\_common\_subtype\_count}: total count of cards belonging to the most frequent subtype in the deck (a tribal proxy).
  \item \texttt{equipment\_subtype\_count}, \texttt{aura\_subtype\_count}, \texttt{vehicle\_subtype\_count}: counts of cards with the corresponding subtype.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Multiface layouts}
\label{app:features:layouts}
\begin{itemize}
  \item \texttt{multiface\_card\_count}: count of cards that expose more than one face.
  \item \texttt{layout\_normal\_count}, \texttt{layout\_transform\_count}, \texttt{layout\_modal\_dfc\_count}, \texttt{layout\_split\_count}, \texttt{layout\_adventure\_count}: per-layout card counts for the tracked layouts.
  \item \texttt{cards\_with\_faces\_count}: count of cards whose \texttt{faces} array is non-empty.
  \item \texttt{total\_face\_count}: total number of faces summed across the deck.
  \item \texttt{max\_faces\_on\_card}: maximum face count observed on any single card.
\end{itemize}

\section{Spot-Checking --- Full Results}
\label{app:spotcheck}

This appendix carries the full spot-checking results, the software versions used, and the default hyperparameters referenced in Section~\ref{sec:spotcheck}.

\paragraph{Software versions.} Python~3.11, \texttt{scikit-learn} 1.5, \texttt{scipy} 1.13, \texttt{numpy} 1.26, \texttt{pandas} 2.2. Hold-out splits use \texttt{StratifiedShuffleSplit} (80/20, 5 seeds) and are reproducible from the seed list $\{1, 2, 3, 4, 5\}$.

\paragraph{Default hyperparameters.} Each candidate runs with its scikit-learn 1.5 defaults, except where the default is not viable on this scale. The notable defaults are:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \texttt{DecisionTreeClassifier}: \texttt{criterion="gini"}, no depth limit.
  \item \texttt{RandomForestClassifier}: 100 trees, \texttt{max\_features="sqrt"}, \texttt{n\_jobs=-1}.
  \item \texttt{HistGradientBoostingClassifier}: \texttt{max\_iter=100}, \texttt{learning\_rate=0.1}, \texttt{max\_leaf\_nodes=31}.
  \item \texttt{MultinomialNB}: \texttt{alpha=1.0}, BC representation.
  \item \texttt{GaussianNB}: \texttt{var\_smoothing=1e-9}, DF representation.
  \item \texttt{LogisticRegression}: \texttt{C=1.0}, \texttt{penalty="l2"}, \texttt{max\_iter=1000}; solver \texttt{lbfgs} on DF and \texttt{saga} on BC.
  \item \texttt{LinearSVC}: \texttt{C=1.0}, \texttt{penalty="l2"}, \texttt{loss="squared\_hinge"}, \texttt{max\_iter=5000}.
  \item \texttt{KNeighborsClassifier}: \texttt{n\_neighbors=5}, \texttt{weights="uniform"}.
\end{itemize}
\texttt{StandardScaler} is applied to DF for the scale-sensitive algorithms; tree-based models and \texttt{GaussianNB} receive unscaled features.
